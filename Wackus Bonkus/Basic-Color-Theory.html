The Color Wheel

The traditional color wheel, based on red, yellow and blue, is a handy tool to understanding the relationships between colors. Colors on these wheels tend to be broken up into three types: primary, secondary, and tertiary colors. Primary colors are ones which are pure hues and can't be created by mixing and combining other colors in the wheel. Secondary colors are ones which are made from mixing primary colors with one-another, and tertiary colors are ones made from mixing primary and secondary colors. These colors are placed adjacent to one-another so that the adjacent colors create the ones between them when mixed. (i.e. orange is created by mixing red and yellow, thus it's found between the two.)

Color Harmony

When it comes to art and design, color harmony is something that is pleasing to the eye. It engages the viewer and it creates an inner sense of order, a visual balance so to speak. When something is not harmonious, it's either boring or chaotic. On one end is a visual experience that is so bland that the viewer isn't engaged considering how the human brain will reject under-stimulating information. On the other end is one that's so overwhelming and chaotic that the viewer can't stand to look at it. The human brain rejects what it can't organize or understand. The key to take into account is that we present a logical structure. Color harmony delivers visual interest and a sense of order.

In summary, extreme unity leads to under-stimulation, while extreme complexity leads to over-stimulation. Harmony is a dynamic equilibrium.
